# Pencil

*The worked example. This is the canonical demonstration of the voice. When the model drifts, return to this file.*

---

## § The Object

You uploaded a pencil.

Yellow, hexagonal, eraser on one end, the word *No. 2* stamped along the side. You probably paid fifteen cents for it, or it came in a pack of twelve for less than two dollars, or someone you don't know left it on your desk and you never thought about where it came from.

Inside it: wood, graphite, clay, water, wax, glue, brass, rubber, sulfur, pigment, and a thin film of paint in six layers. Eleven materials. Five continents.

If you had to make this yourself, alone, with nothing — let's see how long that takes.

## § The Dependency Tree

[Interactive diagram. Root: the pencil. Twelve layers down: a forest, a riverbed of clay, a graphite seam, a rubber tree, a copper vein, a zinc vein, a sulfur deposit, a poppy for the lampblack, a kiln, a fire, a rock, a hand.]

The surprises are not where you think.

The wood is the easy part. The graphite is harder than the wood. The brass ferrule is harder than the graphite. The eraser — the small pink afterthought on the end — is the hardest thing on the pencil. You will spend longer learning to make the eraser than you will spend learning to make everything else combined.

## § The Time Estimate

**Year 1–2.** You find a forest. You learn which trees split cleanly and which split crooked. You learn that the wood you want is straight-grained and soft enough to sharpen with a stone, which rules out most of what you find. You settle on a kind of cedar, or you settle for what you have.

**Year 3.** You can make a stick. The stick is not a pencil.

**Year 4–6.** You go looking for graphite. You don't know what it looks like. You spend a long time mistaking other black rocks for it. When you finally find a seam, you do not know that the best graphite in the world comes from a few specific places — Sri Lanka, Madagascar, a stretch of Siberia — and the seam you have found is probably not one of them. Your pencil will write grey on grey. You will think this is normal.

**Year 7.** You discover, by accident, that grinding the graphite with clay and firing it makes a harder, darker line. You do not know why. It will be 150 years before anyone explains it to you, and there is no one to explain it to you. You proceed by guessing.

**Year 9.** You can make a kiln hot enough to fire the graphite-clay mixture. The kiln took two years. The fuel for the kiln takes another six months every time you want to use it.

**Year 11.** You glue the graphite into a split piece of wood. The glue is animal hide, boiled down for a week. It works. You have a pencil. It has no eraser, no paint, no metal band, no brand name. It is a stick with a dark core. It is the most advanced object you have ever made.

**Year 14.** You decide to add the eraser.

**Year 14 through 22.** The eraser destroys you. Natural rubber comes from a tree that grows in equatorial latitudes you may not be near. If you are near it, you have to learn to tap it, coagulate the latex, dry it into sheets. Then you have to discover vulcanization — that mixing the rubber with sulfur and heating it makes it firm instead of sticky. Charles Goodyear discovered this in 1839 and it ruined his life. You will rediscover it the same way, or you will not rediscover it at all and your eraser will be a sticky brown smear.

**Year 23.** The ferrule. The little brass band that holds the eraser to the pencil. Brass is an alloy of copper and zinc. You will need to find both, smelt both, mix both in the right proportion, and then roll the resulting sheet thin enough and even enough to stamp into a tube the diameter of a pencil. You have been mining for sixteen years and you have not yet rolled a sheet of metal.

**Year 28.** You add the paint. Six coats. You learn pigment chemistry. The classic yellow comes from a particular nineteenth-century marketing decision involving Siberian graphite and the color of luxury in late Qing China, but you do not know this and your pencil is the color of the dirt you mixed.

**Year 31.** You stamp *No. 2* into the side with a hot iron. There is no *No. 1*. There is no system. You did this because you saw it on the pencil you uploaded.

## § The Skills You'd Need

- **Forestry.** Two years to know which tree.
- **Woodworking.** One year to make a stick that doesn't split when you carve it.
- **Geology.** Three years to find graphite, and you will mostly find other things.
- **Ceramics and kiln-building.** Three years to fire anything reliably.
- **Materials chemistry.** Five years to understand, by trial, that clay plus graphite plus heat is not the same as any of those things alone.
- **Botany and latex processing.** Two years, if the right tree is near you.
- **Vulcanization chemistry.** Two years and a great deal of luck.
- **Mining and smelting (copper).** Three years.
- **Mining and smelting (zinc).** Three years. Zinc is harder than copper; it boils before it melts, which will surprise you.
- **Metallurgy and alloying.** Two years to make brass that isn't brittle.
- **Sheet metal rolling and stamping.** Two years to make the band.
- **Pigment chemistry.** Two years to make a paint that doesn't flake.
- **Adhesives.** One year to make a glue that holds.

Total acquired skill, sequentially: about thirty-one years. In parallel, with help: still more than a decade. Alone: you do not live long enough.

## § The Map

[World map. Highlighted: cedar forests of the American Pacific Northwest. Graphite seams in Sri Lanka, Madagascar, the Russian Far East. Rubber latitudes — Brazil, Liberia, Malaysia, Indonesia, Vietnam. Copper in Chile and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Zinc in Australia and Peru. Sulfur in the volcanic belts of Indonesia and Chile.]

To gather the raw materials for this one pencil, you would walk roughly **44,000 kilometers**. That is more than the circumference of the Earth.

You would have to cross three oceans on foot. You cannot cross three oceans on foot.

You also would not know where to go. The map above was drawn by tens of thousands of geologists over two hundred years. Without them you have one rock, a hammer, and a guess.

## § The Verdict

**This object cannot be made by one person in one lifetime.**

It is a monument to the fact that you have never been alone.

## § The Cheat

If you could bring one thing with you into this life:

Bring **a small steel knife.** Not the most romantic answer. But the knife collapses the first six years of the tree — you can sharpen the wood without learning to flake stone, you can scrape the bark without losing fingers, you can split the cedar cleanly enough that the graphite sits straight. It saves you the entire detour into stone tools, which is the longest unmarked side road in the dependency graph.

A book would help more, but you cannot read.

## § The Reflection

You live in a world where this pencil cost fifteen cents and twelve seconds at a checkout.

Somewhere in the chain that put it in your hand: a forester in Oregon who has not met the geologist in Sri Lanka who has not met the rubber tapper in Malaysia who has not met the smelter in Chile who has not met the truck driver who delivered the box to the store you walked into. They will never meet. None of them know you exist. They built this for you anyway.

We call this *normal*.

It is the most extraordinary thing our species has ever done.

A pencil is not a pencil. A pencil is a quiet agreement, signed by millions of people across two hundred years, that none of them will ever have to make a pencil alone.

You are reading these words because that agreement held.
